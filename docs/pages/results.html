Results

This section presents the key findings of our spatial analysis in Serbia from 2021 to 2023. By integrating multi-source geospatial data, we explore the complex relationships between environmental dynamics and human health risks. The following maps, charts, and statistical interpretations are organized into three core areas: Air Quality Change (spatiotemporal trends of NO2, PM2.5, and PM10), Land Cover Change (landscape transitions and stability), and Population Exposure (assessing human vulnerability to pollution hotspots).

Air Quality Change

The Annual Mean Aggregation Change maps show whether pollutant concentrations increased or decreased between 2021 and 2023. These maps are key outputs for interpreting air quality trends in Serbia.

NO₂ AMAC 2021–2023

NO₂ shows a clear decrease across central Serbia, with the strongest reductions between 2 and 5 µg/m³. Slight increases appear mainly in northern and southern peripheral areas.

PM2.5 AMAC 2021–2023

PM2.5 generally decreased across Serbia, especially in the central and southwestern areas. Only a small southern area shows a noticeable increase.

PM10 AMAC 2021–2023

PM10 is mostly stable or slightly decreasing nationwide. The strongest improvement occurs in the southwest, while almost no areas show worsening pollution.

Data Interpretation: Landscape Transitions in Serbia

Based on the 2021–2023 statistics, Serbia shows high overall land cover stability, with all assigned classes retaining more than 92% of their original extent. However, the transition patterns still reveal important landscape dynamics.

  • Built Area: Built-up land remains highly stable, with a 94.3% persistence rate. Most new built-up areas come from Crops, which account for 72.0% of built-area gain. This indicates urban expansion mainly affecting agricultural land.
  • Crops: Cropland has a slightly lower stability rate of 92.9%. Its main transitions are with Rangeland, reflecting shifts between active agriculture, fallow land and grassland areas.
  • Trees: Tree cover is the most stable class, with 97.7% persistence. Forest loss is mainly converted to Rangeland, while most tree gain also comes from Rangeland. This suggests localized vegetation degradation and recovery dynamics.

Zonal Statistics

Zonal statistics compare pollutant concentration change with land cover transition zones. For each pollutant, the mean, minimum and maximum concentration changes were calculated for stable, gain and loss zones of the assigned land cover class. Overall, all three pollutants show a decreasing trend between 2021 and 2023. Since CAMS air quality data has a coarser resolution than the 10 m land cover data, the interpretation mainly focuses on mean values.

NO₂ Zonal Statistics

NO₂ decreased in all built-area transition zones. Stable built-up areas show the smallest mean reduction (-0.74 µg/m³), while gain and loss zones show stronger decreases.

PM2.5 Zonal Statistics

PM2.5 reductions are consistent across cropland zones. The strongest mean decrease occurs where cropland was lost (-1.75 µg/m³), while stable and gained cropland show similar reductions.

PM10 Zonal Statistics

PM10 decreases across all tree-cover transition zones. Stable, gain and loss zones show similar patterns, suggesting a broad regional improvement.

Population Exposure

Human exposure to air pollution depends on both pollutant concentrations and population distribution. By combining these variables, we identify regions where pollution hotspots overlap with densely populated areas, representing the greatest health vulnerability.

Pollution Bivariate Analysis

The bivariate maps combine pollutant concentration and population density to identify zones where air quality exposure is highest. The embedded legend and classification tables explain how pollutant and population classes are coded.

How to Read the Maps

Each GAUL Level 2 administrative region is assigned a two-digit code using zonal statistics. The first digit represents the maximum pollution level within the selected concentration quantile. The second digit represents the median population quantile for the administrative zone. For example, code 55 indicates extreme pollution combined with a highly populated area.

NO₂ Bivariate Exposure

NO₂ exposure is generally low to moderate. Higher exposure is mainly limited to central urban areas where population density is higher.

PM2.5 Bivariate Exposure

PM2.5 shows a higher exposure level than NO₂, with several central and northern zones combining moderate-to-high pollution and population density.

PM10 Bivariate Exposure

PM10 exposure is generally low across Serbia, with most areas falling into the safest bivariate classes, even in more populated zones.

Population Exposure Pie Charts

The pie charts below summarize demographic vulnerability by showing the percentage of the total population exposed to different severity levels of air pollution. Classes 1 to 5 represent increasing maximum pollutant concentration, from low exposure to extreme exposure.

NO₂ Population Exposure

Low Health Risk: This chart indicates that the entire population of Serbia is exposed to relatively safe NO₂ levels. Exactly 54.8% of the population lives in Class 1 (Low) zones, and the remaining 45.2% resides in Class 2 (Moderate-Low) zones. No population is exposed to Class 3 or above.

PM2.5 Population Exposure

Moderate to High Risk: Fine particulate matter presents a significantly higher threat. The vast majority of the population (76.8%) resides in Class 3 (Moderate) pollution zones. More critically, nearly a quarter of the residents are exposed to hazardous levels: 22.1% in Class 4 (High) and 1.1% in Class 5 (Extreme) zones.

PM10 Population Exposure

Low Health Risk: Coarse particulate matter (PM10) poses a minimal health threat in Serbia. An overwhelming 97.3% of the population resides in Class 1 (Low) pollution zones, enjoying safe air quality conditions. Only a tiny fraction (2.69%) experiences Class 2 (Moderate-Low) exposure.

Summary & Conclusions

In summary, the spatial analysis of Serbia (2021–2023) reveals a general improvement in atmospheric conditions, characterized by widespread reductions in PM2.5 and PM10, alongside localized NO₂ improvements in the central regions.

Our land cover assessment indicates a highly stable landscape overall, though significant urban sprawl is evident, with 72.0% of newly built areas encroaching upon agricultural lands. Zonal statistics further highlight that existing stable urban cores experienced the smallest mean reduction in NO₂ (-0.74 µg/m³), indicating that old city centers remain persistent emission hotspots compared to transitioning areas.

Finally, the population exposure assessment exposes a critical public health dichotomy. While NO₂ and coarse particulate matter (PM10) pose minimal to moderate risks to the vast majority of the country, fine particulate matter (PM2.5) emerges as the primary environmental hazard. With nearly a quarter of the population exposed to high or extreme PM2.5 concentrations, these findings emphasize the urgent need for targeted environmental policies, stringent emission controls, and sustainable urban planning to mitigate respiratory health risks across Serbia.
